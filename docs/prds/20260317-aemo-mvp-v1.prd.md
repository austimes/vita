# VedaLang MVP Requirements v1 for AEMO 2027 IASR Support

## Purpose

This document defines **version 1 of the minimum viable product requirements** for the VedaLang multi-sector model intended to replace the current AusTIMES role in supporting AEMO’s **2027 Inputs, Assumptions and Scenarios Report (IASR)** work.

The framing is intentionally **question-first**:

1. **What questions must the model be able to answer?**
2. **What model structure and granularity are required to answer them credibly?**

Throughout this document, **“the model” means a set of coordinated scenario runs and paired sensitivity runs**, not merely a single optimization solve. The MVP therefore needs to support **scenario differencing, lever attribution, and narrative decomposition**, not just point estimates.

---

## 1. Scope of the MVP

The MVP is not intended to be a full replacement for every AusTIMES use case. It is intended to be the **minimum credible multi-sector model** needed to support AEMO’s IASR-related needs.

The MVP must therefore do four things well:

1. **Project energy demand trajectories** across major carriers and regions.
2. **Explain why demand changes** across scenarios and sensitivities.
3. **Quantify cross-carrier coupling**, especially electricity–gas–hydrogen interactions.
4. **Support power-system-facing narratives**, especially the **power-sector emissions / carbon-budget envelope** implied by non-power-sector assumptions.

The MVP should favor **clarity, auditability, and explainability** over unnecessary endogenous detail.

---

## 2. Primary questions the MVP model must answer

### Q1. Total energy demand by carrier, region, and time

The model must answer:

- What is total final energy demand over time under each IASR scenario?
- How does that demand split across major energy carriers:
  - electricity
  - fossil gas
  - biomethane
  - hydrogen
  - liquid fuels
  - coal / coke and other fossil fuels where still materially relevant
- How does that demand vary by region?
- How does total demand change over time under alternative IASR scenarios?

### Q2. Driver attribution of demand change

The model must answer:

- How much of the change in demand for each carrier is attributable to:
  - demand-side activity growth or decline
  - energy efficiency improvement
  - fuel switching / electrification
  - technology adoption constraints / stock turnover
  - policy settings
  - exogenous cost assumptions
  - hydrogen boundary assumptions
  - data-centre growth assumptions
- For electricity specifically, how much incremental demand is attributable to:
  - buildings electrification
  - EV uptake and charging assumptions
  - industrial electrification
  - in-scope hydrogen electrolysis
  - data centres
  - behind-the-meter supply effects interacting with grid demand

### Q3. Electricity demand trajectory and directional load-shape implications

The model must answer:

- What is total electricity demand by region over time under each scenario?
- What are the major drivers of scenario differences?
- How do demand shapes move directionally under electrification and EV uptake?
- Is the direction of change consistent with the narratives AEMO needs for IASR, even without dispatch-grade fidelity?
- What is the role of behind-the-meter rooftop PV and batteries in changing grid demand, gross load, and residual demand shape?

### Q4. Gas demand, substitution, and phase-down narrative

The model must answer:

- What is delivered gas demand by region over time under each scenario?
- How much gas demand reduction comes from:
  - electrification
  - efficiency
  - fuel switching to biomethane
  - fuel switching to hydrogen where included
  - policy constraints such as no-new-gas-connections
- Under a gas phase-down or no-new-gas-connections lever, what demand shifts to electricity and what remains on gaseous fuels?

### Q5. Hydrogen production, use, and electricity coupling

The model must answer:

- How much hydrogen is produced and consumed in each scenario?
- What share is produced from in-scope grid- or REZ-connected electrolysis?
- What share is treated as out-of-scope or exogenous?
- How sensitive are electricity demand and emissions outcomes to hydrogen boundary assumptions?
- Is hydrogen demand always matched by a physically consistent supply representation?

### Q6. Transport transition and liquid-fuels decline

The model must answer:

- What is the trajectory of liquid-fuels demand over time?
- How much of that change is driven by:
  - light vehicle electrification
  - freight transition
  - efficiency improvement
  - hydrogen uptake where represented
  - other exogenous transport trends
- What is the implied electricity demand from transport under each scenario?
- What charging-shape assumptions materially change the electricity narrative?

### Q7. Industry transition, with minimum credible sectoral story

The model must answer:

- What is industrial final energy by carrier over time?
- What are the main levers driving industrial switching?
- What is the minimum heat story that explains the transition:
  - low / medium temperature heat
  - high temperature heat
- What is happening in the named industrial narratives AEMO is likely to care about, at minimum:
  - cement
  - alumina
  - iron and steel
- What is happening for large industrial loads and safeguard-covered facilities versus the rest of industry?

### Q8. Large industrial loads and safeguard-facility narrative

The model must answer:

- What are the energy and emissions trajectories for major safeguard-linked facilities or facility classes?
- How much of electricity demand growth, gas demand change, hydrogen demand, and emissions change is coming from:
  - named large industrial facilities / sectors
  - other safeguard facilities
  - non-safeguard industry
- Can the model tell a credible story for facility-level transition archetypes without fully bespoke engineering models for every site?

### Q9. Fossil fuel exports and LNG narrative

The model must answer:

- What is the fossil fuel exports narrative over time, especially for LNG?
- How are LNG export and other fossil-fuel-export assumptions treated for emissions accounting and IASR storytelling?
- If gas or LNG feedgas is not fully in AEMO’s operational demand boundary, how is it still reflected in emissions and carbon-budget logic?
- How do fossil-fuel-export assumptions affect the residual emissions envelope available to the power sector?

### Q10. Emissions by sector, driver, and consistency check

The model must answer:

- What are emissions outcomes by major sector under each scenario, at minimum:
  - buildings
  - transport
  - industry
  - fossil fuel exports / processing where included for accounting
- How do those emissions changes decompose into:
  - reduced fossil combustion
  - electrification / fuel switching
  - efficiency
  - low-carbon gas substitution
  - exogenous demand change
- Are there internal consistency checks that identify narrative breaks, for example:
  - hydrogen demand without corresponding electricity or out-of-scope supply
  - gas decline without replacement energy
  - rooftop PV or batteries reducing grid demand without explicit supply representation
  - power-sector carbon budget implication inconsistent with total economy story

### Q11. Power-system carbon budget / emissions envelope

The model must answer:

- What residual emissions envelope or carbon budget is implied for the power system under each IASR scenario?
- What uncertainty range surrounds that number under key non-power-sector assumptions?
- How do changes in buildings, transport, industry, hydrogen, and fossil-fuel-export assumptions change the remaining power-sector budget?
- At what granularity can that budget or emissions envelope be reported:
  - national
  - by electricity region where appropriate
  - by driver / scenario delta

### Q12. Consultation levers and marginal sensitivities

For each IASR scenario, the model must answer:

- What is the marginal impact of key sensitivities on electricity, gas, hydrogen, liquid fuels, and emissions?
- The MVP must support paired delta runs for at least:
  - faster / slower buildings electrification
  - faster / slower industrial electrification
  - EV adoption high / low
  - managed charging on / off
  - data-centre growth high / low
  - hydrogen in-scope share high / low
  - biomethane availability high / low
  - efficiency high / low
  - gas connection policy on / off
  - fossil-fuel-export trajectory high / low where relevant to carbon-budget logic

## 3. Summary question hierarchy

The MVP question set can be collapsed into three top-level questions.

### Top-level question A

**What is energy demand over time across all major energy carriers, regions, and sectors under each IASR scenario?**

### Top-level question B

**Why does that demand change?**
This is the most important requirement. The model must attribute change to demand-side effects, efficiency, fuel switching, policy, technology uptake, cost assumptions, and boundary assumptions.

### Top-level question C

**What emissions and residual power-sector carbon-budget implications follow from those scenario assumptions?**

Everything else in the MVP should be justified by whether it is needed to answer one of those three top-level questions.

## 4. Model structure required to answer those questions

## 4.1 Core design principle

The minimum viable structure is:

- **rich enough to explain** demand and emissions changes,
- **coarse enough to remain auditable and fast**, and
- **explicit enough to prevent non-physical storytelling**, especially around hydrogen, behind-the-meter supply, and carbon-budget implications.

The core structural dimensions are:

1. **time**
2. **space**
3. **sector / subsector**
4. **end use / service demand**
5. **technology / supply option**
6. **energy carrier**
7. **scenario / lever state**

---

## 5. Proposed v1 granularity specification

## 5.1 Temporal granularity table

| Dimension                      | Proposed v1                                                                                                                       | Purpose                                                     | Why this is minimum credible                                                                 |
| ------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Model years                    | Annual from base year to 2050                                                                                                     | Main IASR pathway reporting                                 | Annual resolution is sufficient for scenario, trend, and policy narratives                   |
| Intra-annual slices            | 8 slices = Summer Day, Summer Night, Winter Day, Winter Night, Shoulder-1 Day, Shoulder-1 Night, Shoulder-2 Day, Shoulder-2 Night | Directional demand-shape impacts only                       | Enough to capture seasonality and day/night effects without implying dispatch-grade fidelity |
| Optional additional peak slice | Not included in v1 base case; held as design option                                                                               | Only if AEMO explicitly requires peak-directional reporting | Keeps MVP lean unless AEMO needs stronger peak narrative                                     |
| Vintage / stock timing         | Annual stock turnover or annual adoption bounds                                                                                   | Capture appliance and vehicle replacement pacing            | Necessary to avoid implausible instantaneous switching                                       |

### Temporal design notes

- The model is **not** intended to be a dispatch or capacity-expansion power model.
- Time slices exist only to support **directional shape impacts**, especially for:
  - EV charging
  - space conditioning electrification
  - rooftop PV and behind-the-meter batteries
  - data-centre load shape
  - electrolysis timing assumptions where required
- AEMO should explicitly confirm whether **any time slicing is needed at all**.

## 5.5 Technology-granularity principle

The MVP should use **technology archetypes**, not maximal technology detail.

The objective is to represent only as much technology detail as is required to answer:

- what carrier demand changes,
- why it changes,
- which policy or cost lever caused it, and
- what the emissions and power-budget implications are.

That means:

- use **named technologies** where the narrative depends on them,
- use **archetype buckets** where the narrative does not.

Examples:

- Residential cooking can remain **gas cooking vs electric cooking**.
- Freight can remain **diesel vs electric vs optional hydrogen**.
- Industry can use **LMH / HTH archetypes** for most loads, but **cement / alumina / iron & steel / LNG** require named sector narratives.

## 6. Minimum model modules required

## 6.1 Buildings module

Must support:

- gas-to-electric switching
- efficiency improvements
- rooftop PV and behind-the-meter battery adoption
- no-new-gas-connections policy
- electricity demand impacts by region and time slice

## 6.2 Transport module

Must support:

- light-road electrification
- freight transition
- optional hydrogen freight sensitivity
- charging-shape assumptions
- liquid-fuels decline reporting

## 6.3 Industry module

Must support:

- LMH / HTH switching logic
- facility-level or facility-class treatment for large safeguard-linked loads
- named narrative treatment for cement, alumina, iron and steel
- fossil-fuel-export and LNG-related energy / emissions logic where in scope for accounting

## 6.4 Data-centre module

Must support:

- region-specific electricity demand trajectories
- flat or baseload-like shape assumption
- independent high / low sensitivity

## 6.5 Hydrogen boundary module

Must support:

- explicit in-scope electrolysis
- explicit out-of-scope hydrogen supply
- unambiguous mapping between hydrogen demand and supply source
- reporting split between in-scope and out-of-scope hydrogen

## 6.6 Behind-the-meter supply module

Must support:

- rooftop PV as explicit supply
- behind-the-meter batteries as explicit storage / supply
- reporting of gross demand, behind-the-meter supply, and residual grid demand

## 6.7 Emissions and carbon-budget module

Must support:

- direct emissions by sector and carrier
- optional process-emissions proxies where needed
- fossil-fuel-export / LNG accounting as required for narrative
- derivation of residual power-sector emissions envelope consistent with scenario assumptions

## 7. Exogenous interfaces for the MVP

To keep the MVP tractable, the following should be treated as exogenous scenario inputs rather than endogenous sub-models.

| Input class                                                      | Proposed v1 treatment                                       |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Electricity price by region and year                             | Exogenous                                                   |
| Electricity emissions intensity or power-sector emissions factor | Exogenous, unless replaced by a linked power-model workflow |
| Gas price by region and year                                     | Exogenous                                                   |
| Liquid-fuels price trajectory                                    | Exogenous                                                   |
| Biomethane supply potential / maximum availability               | Exogenous                                                   |
| Hydrogen demand emergence outside direct endogenous logic        | Exogenous scenario lever where needed                       |
| Data-centre growth trajectories                                  | Exogenous                                                   |
| Fossil-fuel-export throughput assumptions                        | Exogenous                                                   |
| National emissions target / carbon-budget framing                | Exogenous policy input                                      |

### Exogenous-interface principle

The MVP should not attempt to become a full electricity-market model. Unless AEMO explicitly requires otherwise, the multi-sector model should consume simplified power-system-facing inputs rather than build a full endogenous power sector.

## 8. Minimum reporting schema

The reporting layer is not optional. It is part of the MVP requirements.

## 8.1 Core output tables

| Output table                       | Minimum dimensions                                                       |
| ---------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Final energy demand by carrier     | scenario × year × region × sector × carrier                              |
| Electricity demand by driver       | scenario × year × region × driver                                        |
| Electricity load shape summary     | scenario × year × region × time slice × driver                           |
| Behind-the-meter supply summary    | scenario × year × region × sector × rooftop PV / battery output          |
| Gas demand summary                 | scenario × year × region × sector × fossil gas / biomethane              |
| Hydrogen summary                   | scenario × year × region × produced / consumed × in-scope / out-of-scope |
| Liquid-fuels summary               | scenario × year × region × transport subsector                           |
| Industry energy summary            | scenario × year × region × facility / subsector × carrier                |
| Emissions summary                  | scenario × year × region × sector × driver                               |
| Power-sector carbon-budget summary | scenario × year × national or region × residual power-sector envelope    |
| Delta report                       | scenario-pair × year × region × sector × carrier × driver                |

## 8.2 Required narrative decompositions

The MVP must be able to generate standardized decompositions for at least:

- electricity demand growth by driver
- gas demand decline by cause
- hydrogen demand and supply split by boundary status
- liquid-fuels decline by transport driver
- industrial energy switching by sector or facility class
- emissions change by sector and mechanism
- power-sector residual carbon-budget change by non-power driver

## 11. MVP boundaries: what is deliberately not in v1

The following are intentionally out of scope unless AEMO requires them.

- Full power-system optimization or dispatch
- Detailed transmission representation
- Detailed industrial process simulation for every facility
- Detailed non-road transport technology competition beyond what is needed for carrier totals
- High-resolution subregional spatial modelling within each electricity region
- Full hourly or sub-hourly chronology

These can be added later only if they are shown to be necessary to answer AEMO’s actual questions.

## 12. Recommended v1 answer to the granularity question

### Recommended v1 position

The MVP should be built with:

- **annual years to 2050**
- **8 intra-annual slices** as the default proposed design, but explicitly subject to AEMO confirmation
- **Australia-wide electricity-network regionality**, consisting of:
  - QLD
  - NSW/ACT
  - VIC
  - SA
  - TAS
  - SWIS
  - NWIS
  - NT regulated system
  - Off-grid Australia
- **sectoral detail sufficient to separate**:
  - buildings
  - transport
  - data centres
  - facility-level large industry / safeguard-linked loads
  - rest-of-industry
- **technology detail only where needed to explain switching and emissions outcomes**

This is the proposed v1 minimum credible granularity.

## 13. Questions for AEMO

The main purpose of the next AEMO check-in should be to validate whether this granularity is sufficient, too detailed, or missing critical narrative needs.

### 13.1 Core granularity questions

1. **Is the proposed spatial granularity acceptable?**

   - NEM split into QLD, NSW/ACT, VIC, SA, TAS
   - SWIS, NWIS, NT regulated system, and Off-grid represented separately
   - Do you want anything coarser or finer?

2. **Do you need intra-annual time slices at all?**

   - If yes, are 8 slices sufficient?
   - If not, can all IASR-relevant outputs be annual only?

3. **Do you require explicit representation of behind-the-meter rooftop PV and batteries as supply?**

   - Our recommendation is yes, explicitly.
   - Is that aligned with how you want to see the story told?

### 13.2 Power-system-boundary questions

4. **Do you need any representation of the power system beyond exogenous electricity prices and an electricity emissions intensity / emissions envelope input?**

   - If yes, what specific outputs are needed that cannot be supplied externally?

5. **How do you want the power-sector carbon budget / emissions envelope expressed?**

   - National only?
   - By electricity region?
   - As a residual from economy-wide assumptions?
   - As a range rather than a point estimate?

### 13.3 Hydrogen-boundary questions

6. **How should hydrogen be classified as in-scope versus out-of-scope for IASR purposes?**

   - Grid-connected electrolysis?
   - REZ-connected electrolysis?
   - Dedicated off-grid renewables?
   - Imported / exogenous hydrogen?

7. **Do you need hydrogen consumption shown by end-use sector, or is total in-scope electricity demand from electrolysis the key output?**

### 13.4 Gas and export-boundary questions

8. **Do you need gas for power and LNG feedgas represented explicitly within the IASR support model, or only as exogenous accounting / narrative items?**

9. **How do you want fossil fuel exports, especially LNG, treated in the emissions and carbon-budget story?**

   - Fully inside the accounting boundary?
   - Exogenous throughput with emissions consequences?
   - Narrative-only?

### 13.5 Industry-detail questions

10. **Which industrial sectors or named facility classes must be visible in IASR narratives?**

    - Is cement, alumina, and iron & steel sufficient?
    - Do aluminium, LNG, ammonia, chemicals, or other safeguard sectors also need named treatment?

11. **Do you want large industrial loads represented as individual facilities, facility classes, or only sector aggregates?**

### 13.6 Sensitivity-design questions

12. **Are the proposed paired sensitivities the right consultation lever set?**

    - electrification pace
    - EV uptake and charging
    - data-centre growth
    - hydrogen boundary share
    - biomethane availability
    - efficiency
    - gas-connection policy
    - fossil-fuel-export trajectory

13. **Which of these sensitivities must be core IASR consultation levers versus secondary analytical levers?**

## 14. Recommended immediate next step

Use this document as the **v1 requirements baseline** for the next AEMO check-in.

At that meeting, seek explicit feedback on only four things:

1. **Question set** — are these the questions they actually need answered?
2. **Granularity** — is the proposed time / space / sector / tech detail sufficient?
3. **Boundary choices** — especially hydrogen, behind-the-meter supply, and fossil-fuel exports.
4. **Power-system interface** — do they need more than exogenous electricity price and emissions inputs?

Once those are confirmed, the next design artifact should be:

- a **formal VedaLang granularity schema**, and
- a **draft MVP reference energy system structure** aligned to that schema.

---

## 15. Bottom line

The proposed v1 MVP is built around a simple principle:

**The model must be able to quantify demand, explain demand change, and derive the power-sector-facing emissions implications — without hand-waving and without unnecessary endogenous complexity.**

That is the standard the v1 design should be judged
