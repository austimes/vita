# VedaLang MVP Requirements v3 for AEMO 2027 IASR Support

## Purpose

This document defines the current working version of the minimum viable product requirements for the VedaLang multi-sector model intended to support AEMO’s 2027 Inputs, Assumptions and Scenarios Report (IASR) work.

It is focused on two things only:

1. the questions the model must answer
2. the minimum reference energy system structure and granularity required to answer them

The document is therefore about the **model structure itself**.

---

## 1. Core questions the MVP must answer

### 1.1 Energy demand over time

The model must answer:

- What is energy demand over time across the major energy carriers under each IASR scenario?
- What causes changes in that demand over time and across scenarios?
- Which sectors, subsectors, end uses, technologies, and large industrial loads are driving those changes?

The main causes of change that the model must be able to represent are:

- demand-side activity change
- energy efficiency
- fuel switching and electrification
- technology switching
- policy settings
- cost assumptions
- hydrogen boundary assumptions
- data-centre growth
- fossil fuel export assumptions where material to energy use or emissions

### 1.2 Power-system emissions budget

The model must answer:

- What residual emissions budget or emissions envelope is implied for the power system under each IASR scenario?
- How does that budget change under different non-power-sector assumptions?
- Which assumptions and levers affect that budget the most?

This is the core reason the multi-sector model is needed: to quantify how assumptions outside the power sector affect the emissions budget that remains for the power system.

## 2. Proposed v1 granularity specification

## 2.1 Temporal granularity

The temporal resolution should be denser in the near term and progressively coarser further out, in line with rising uncertainty.

| Horizon segment | Proposed v1 specification |
| --- | --- |
| Near term | Annual from the present year to 2035 |
| Medium term | 2-year steps thereafter |
| Long term | 5-year steps thereafter |
| Very long term | 10-year steps thereafter |

## 2.2 Spatial granularity

The proposed base spatial granularity is electricity-network level.

| Spatial layer | Proposed v1 regions |
| --- | --- |
| Electricity-network regions | NEM, SWIS, NWIS, DKIS, Off-grid |

All underlying data should be mapped into these regions.

## 2.3 Sector, subsector, end-use, and technology granularity

| Sector | Proposed v1 subsectors / categories | Proposed v1 end uses / services | Proposed v1 technology detail |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Buildings | Residential, Commercial | Space conditioning, water heating, cooking, other electricity | Gas, electric, heat pump where relevant; behind-the-meter supply shown explicitly where included |
| Transport | Light road, freight road, other transport aggregate | Mobility / freight service proxies and exogenous transport demand where needed | ICE, BEV, optional hydrogen freight |
| Industry | Named large industrial / safeguard-linked facility archetypes; rest of industry | Low/medium temperature heat, high temperature heat, non-heat electricity where needed | Generic facility or process archetypes, not bespoke site engineering models |
| Data centres | Standalone category | Electricity demand | Exogenous regional demand trajectory |
| Hydrogen supply and use | In-scope production, off-grid supply, other out-of-scope supply, final use | Hydrogen production and final demand | Explicit distinction between in-scope and off-grid / out-of-scope supply |
| Fossil fuel exports / processing | LNG and other export-related activity where material | Energy use and emissions linkage | Simplified explicit representation and/or exogenous activity assumptions |

## 2.4 Named industry detail required in v1

At minimum, the industrial narrative should be able to distinguish:

- cement
- alumina
- aluminium
- iron and steel
- LNG / gas processing
- ammonia where material
- other major safeguard-linked industrial loads as generic archetype groups
- the rest of industry as an aggregated residual category

## 2.5 Facility granularity principle

Where facility-level narrative is needed, the MVP should use generic facility archetypes or facility classes rather than bespoke engineering representations of individual sites.

The purpose is to represent major transition pathways and their implications for energy use, emissions, and safeguard-related narratives.

## 3. Minimum reference energy system requirements

## 3.1 Core dimensions

The reference energy system must support the following dimensions:

- year
- region
- sector
- subsector / facility class
- end use / service
- technology / process route
- energy carrier
- scenario / assumption set

## 3.2 Core carrier representation

The reference energy system must include, at minimum, the energy carriers needed to tell the IASR demand and emissions story:

- electricity
- fossil gas
- biomethane
- hydrogen
- liquid fuels
- coal, coke, and other fossil fuels where material to industrial energy use or emissions

Behind-the-meter supply should be represented explicitly where included, rather than appearing only as an unexplained demand reduction.

## 3.3 Buildings representation

The model must represent buildings well enough to capture:

- gas-to-electric switching
- efficiency improvements
- rooftop PV and behind-the-meter supply where included
- policy settings such as no new gas connections where relevant

## 3.4 Transport representation

The model must represent transport well enough to capture:

- light-road electrification
- freight transition
- liquid-fuels decline
- transport electricity demand growth

## 3.5 Industry representation

The model must represent industry well enough to capture:

- switching between major energy carriers
- the distinction between large industrial / safeguard-linked loads and the rest of industry
- major process-route shifts where they matter to the narrative
- the minimum credible heat story through low/medium temperature heat and high temperature heat

## 3.6 Hydrogen boundary representation

The model must represent hydrogen explicitly enough to distinguish between:

- hydrogen produced within scope
- hydrogen supplied from off-grid sources
- hydrogen supplied from other out-of-scope sources

This is needed so that hydrogen demand is physically and geographically accounted for within the model structure.

## 3.7 Fossil fuel exports and LNG representation

The model must include a simplified explicit treatment of fossil fuel exports, especially LNG, wherever they materially affect:

- energy use
- emissions outcomes
- the residual emissions budget available to the power system

Export-related activity should therefore be able to enter the model as a driver of energy use and emissions through exogenous assumptions and simplified explicit representation.

## 3.8 Power-system interface

The MVP should represent electricity as a purchased energy carrier linked to exogenous power-sector-facing assumptions such as electricity price and electricity emissions intensity.

The key boundary issue is whether the multi-sector model also needs to account explicitly for fuel inputs to electricity generation, such as gas and coal used in the power sector, or whether those remain external assumptions supplied alongside the electricity assumptions.

## 4. Questions for AEMO

The next AEMO discussion should focus on confirming the scope and granularity of the MVP and clarifying the main model-boundary decisions.

### 4.1 Core purpose

1. Are the two core questions above the right ones for the MVP?
   - energy demand over time and what causes it
   - the implied power-system emissions budget and what affects it

### 4.2 Temporal granularity

2. Is the proposed temporal resolution suitable for the MVP?
   - annual from the present year to 2035
   - then progressively coarser time steps thereafter
3. Do you need any intra-annual temporal representation in the multi-sector model at all?
4. If yes, what specific output or decision requires it?

### 4.3 Spatial granularity

5. Is electricity-network-level regionality sufficient for the MVP?
   - NEM
   - SWIS
   - NWIS
   - DKIS
   - Off-grid

### 4.4 Power-system boundary

6. Is it acceptable for the MVP to treat electricity as an exogenous carrier characterised by electricity price and electricity emissions assumptions, while AEMO continues to handle detailed power-system modelling separately?
7. Does the MVP need to explicitly account for power-sector fuel inputs such as gas and coal, or can those remain external assumptions where needed for consistency of total energy and emissions accounting?

### 4.5 Behind-the-meter supply

8. Do you want behind-the-meter batteries shown explicitly in the model structure?

### 4.6 Industry detail

9. Beyond cement, alumina, aluminium, iron and steel, LNG / gas processing, and ammonia, are there any other industrial categories that must be explicitly visible in the MVP narrative?

### 4.7 Hydrogen boundary

10. Is the proposed hydrogen boundary treatment sufficient for MVP purposes?
    - in-scope production
    - off-grid supply
    - other out-of-scope supply

---

## 5. Bottom line

The base MVP should stay tightly focused.

It needs to answer two questions well:

1. What is the energy demand over time, and what causes it?
2. What is the implied emissions budget for the power system, and what affects it?

The proposed granularity and reference energy system structure above are intended to be the minimum needed to answer those questions credibly while keeping the detailed power-system modelling boundary with AEMO.
