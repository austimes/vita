# Multi-Sector Model MVP Requirements v6 for AEMO 2027 IASR Support

## Purpose

This document defines the current working version of the minimum viable product requirements for the multi-sector model intended to support AEMO’s 2027 Inputs, Assumptions and Scenarios Report (IASR) work.

It is focused on two things only:

1. the questions the model must answer
2. the minimum reference energy system structure and granularity required to answer them

The document is therefore about the model structure itself.

---

## 1. Core questions the MVP must answer

### 1.1 Energy demand over time

The model must answer:

- What is energy demand over time across the major energy carriers under each IASR scenario?
- What causes changes in that demand over time and across scenarios?
- Which sectors, subsectors, end uses, technologies, and large industrial loads are driving those changes?

The main causes of change that the model must be able to represent are:

- demand-side activity change
- energy efficiency
- fuel switching and electrification
- technology switching
- policy settings
- cost assumptions
- hydrogen boundary assumptions
- data centres
- fossil fuel exports

### 1.2 Power-system emissions budget

The model must answer:

- What residual emissions budget or emissions envelope is implied for the power system under each IASR scenario?
- How does that budget change under different non-power-sector assumptions?
- Which assumptions and levers affect that budget the most?

This is the core reason the multi-sector model is needed: to quantify how assumptions outside the power sector affect the emissions budget that remains for the power system.

## 2. Proposed v1 granularity specification

### 2.1 Temporal granularity

The temporal resolution should be finer in the near term and progressively coarser further out.

The guiding principle is that the model should retain annual resolution only while that level of precision is warranted by the decision context and the uncertainty in the outlook. Beyond that point, the model should move to larger time steps in order to reduce run time without losing meaningful analytical value.

An indicative implementation might begin with annual steps, then move to 2-year, 5-year, and eventually 10-year spacing, but the exact cut-over points should remain flexible.

### 2.2 Spatial granularity

The proposed base spatial granularity is electricity-network level.

| Spatial layer | Proposed v1 regions |
| --- | --- |
| Electricity-network regions | NEM, SWIS, NWIS, DKIS, Off-grid |

All underlying data should be mapped into these regions.

### 2.4 Large industrial load detail required in v1

The model should incorporate explicit sectoral or facility-class representation for the large industrial loads that matter most to the IASR narrative.

This should cover the major energy users, major emitters, or both, without trying to define an exhaustive fixed list up front.

Examples include cement, alumina, aluminium, iron and steel, ammonia, mining and minerals processing, oil and gas processing, and other major safeguard-linked industrial or resource loads.

All other activity can sit in aggregated residual categories for the rest of industry and the rest of resources.

### 2.5 Facility granularity principle

Where facility-level narrative is needed, the MVP should use generic facility archetypes or facility classes rather than bespoke engineering representations of individual sites.

The purpose is to represent major transition pathways and their implications for energy use, emissions, and safeguard-related narratives.

## 3. Minimum reference energy system requirements

### 3.1 Core dimensions

The reference energy system must support the following dimensions:

- year
- region
- sector
- subsector / facility class
- end use / service
- technology / process route
- energy carrier
- scenario / assumption set

### 3.2 Core carrier representation

The reference energy system must include, at minimum, the energy carriers needed to tell the IASR demand and emissions story:

- electricity
- natural gas
- biomethane
- hydrogen
- liquid fuels
- coal, coke, and other fossil fuels where relevant to sectoral energy use or emissions

Behind-the-meter supply should be represented explicitly where included, rather than appearing only as an unexplained demand reduction.

### 3.3 Built environment representation

The model must represent the built environment well enough to capture:

- natural-gas-to-electric switching
- efficiency improvements
- rooftop PV and behind-the-meter supply where included
- policy settings such as no new gas connections where relevant
- explicit data-centre demand as a distinct built-environment subsector

### 3.4 Transport representation

The model must represent transport well enough to capture:

- light-road electrification
- freight transition
- liquid-fuels decline
- transport electricity demand growth

### 3.5 Industry representation

The model must represent industry well enough to capture:

- switching between major energy carriers
- the distinction between large industrial loads and the rest of industry
- major process-route shifts where they matter to the narrative
- the minimum credible heat story through low/medium temperature heat and high temperature heat

### 3.6 Resources representation

The model must represent the resources sector well enough to capture:

- mining and processing energy use where relevant
- fossil fuel export activity
- export-related processing and compression where relevant
- the interaction between resource-sector assumptions, emissions outcomes, and the residual power-system emissions budget

### 3.7 AFOLU representation

The model must represent AFOLU well enough to capture:

- direct energy use where relevant
- sectoral emissions where relevant to the overall emissions budget
- land-based removals where they are needed for whole-of-economy consistency

### 3.8 Hydrogen representation

The model must distinguish between:

- hydrogen production represented inside the model boundary
- hydrogen supplied from the Off-grid region
- other exogenous hydrogen supply

This is needed so that hydrogen demand is physically and geographically accounted for within the model structure.

### 3.9 Fossil fuel exports representation

Fossil fuel exports should be treated explicitly in the model structure through exogenous activity assumptions and simplified sector representation.

### 3.10 Power-system interface

The MVP should represent electricity through exogenous assumptions that vary by year and scenario, at minimum:

- electricity price
- electricity emissions intensity

These assumptions may be represented as discrete scenario cases or as a structured relationship between price, emissions intensity, and year.

A practical starting point would be to construct a price-versus-emissions-intensity-versus-year relationship using the methodology and framing of GenCost, even if the first implementation is a simplified interpolation between present-day conditions and longer-term endpoints.

Where whole-of-economy consistency requires it, an external mapping should translate electricity emissions-intensity assumptions into implied power-sector fuel use, such as natural gas and coal inputs, for post-processing or linked accounting.

## 4. Questions for AEMO

The next AEMO discussion should focus on the main model-boundary decisions rather than implementation detail.

1. Does the multi-sector model need any intra-annual temporal representation at all?
2. If yes, what specific output or decision requires it?
3. Is electricity-network-level spatial granularity sufficient for the MVP, and can SWIS and NWIS be collapsed to WA if needed?
4. Is it acceptable for the MVP to treat electricity through exogenous year-specific assumptions for price and emissions intensity, while detailed power-system modelling remains with AEMO?
5. For consistency of total energy and emissions accounting, does AEMO need an explicit external mapping from electricity emissions-intensity assumptions to implied power-sector fuel use, such as natural gas and coal inputs?

---

## 5. Bottom line

The base MVP should stay tightly focused.

It needs to answer two questions well:

1. What is the energy demand over time, and what causes it?
2. What is the implied emissions budget for the power system, and what affects it?

The proposed granularity and reference energy system structure above are intended to be the minimum needed to answer those questions credibly while keeping the detailed power-system modelling boundary with AEMO.
